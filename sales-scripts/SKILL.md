# Sales Scripts & Closing Techniques for Local Service Businesses

This skill provides complete sales frameworks, phone scripts, in-home presentation talk tracks, Good-Better-Best pricing strategies, objection handling, upsell techniques, closing methods, and roleplay practice for local service businesses including HVAC, plumbing, roofing, electrical, pest control, and landscaping.

---

## 1. Initial Assessment

Before writing or customizing any sales scripts, gather context about the business.

### Check Existing Data First

1. Look for `product-marketing-context` skill — it may already have brand voice, positioning, differentiators, and target market info.
2. Look for `business/config.json` or any stored business profile — may have services, service area, pricing tiers, team info.
3. Check any previous conversations about the business's sales process.

### Discovery Questions (Ask If Not Already Known)

Ask these questions to customize scripts properly:

- **What services do you offer?** (HVAC, plumbing, roofing, electrical, pest control, landscaping, or a combination)
- **What's your average ticket size?** (Helps calibrate pricing language and financing thresholds)
- **What's your current close rate?** (If they don't know, that's useful info too — means they're not tracking)
- **Who does the selling?** Options:
  - Owner runs every call (common in businesses under $1M)
  - Technicians sell in the field (tech-driven model)
  - Dedicated sales team / comfort advisors (companies over $2M typically)
  - Hybrid: tech diagnoses, salesperson closes bigger jobs
- **What's the biggest objection you hear?** (Price, trust, spouse, competitor quotes, "thinking about it")
- **Do you currently present options or give single-number quotes?** (Critical — single-number quotes leave money on the table)
- **Do you offer financing?** (If not, strongly recommend it — increases close rate 15-20% on jobs over $2,000)

### Tailoring the Approach

Once you have answers, customize scripts based on:

| Factor | How It Changes the Script |
|--------|--------------------------|
| Owner sells | Scripts should be conversational, authentic — they ARE the brand |
| Tech sells | Scripts must be simpler, repeatable, structured — techs aren't natural salespeople |
| Dedicated salesperson | Full presentation framework, GBB mandatory, roleplay training |
| Low close rate (<30%) | Focus on discovery questions and objection handling first |
| High average ticket (>$5K) | Financing language becomes critical in every script |
| Emergency-heavy business | Speed-to-lead scripts, after-hours scripts, urgency language |

## 2. Core Sales Principles

These principles underpin every script and technique in this skill. They are non-negotiable.

### The 70/30 Rule

Listen 70% of the time. Talk 30%. The customer should be doing most of the talking during discovery. Your job is to ask great questions, not deliver a monologue.

Why: People buy from people who understand their problem. You can't understand a problem you haven't heard described. The more a customer talks, the more they convince themselves they need a solution.

### Sell Outcomes, Not Equipment

Homeowners don't care about SEER ratings, pipe gauges, or shingle thickness. They care about:

- **Comfort** — "Your family will actually be comfortable upstairs for the first time"
- **Safety** — "This eliminates the carbon monoxide risk we found"
- **Savings** — "This will cut your energy bill by about 30%"
- **Peace of mind** — "You won't have to worry about this for the next 15 years"
- **Convenience** — "We handle everything — permits, inspection, cleanup"

Translate every feature into a benefit the customer can feel.

| Instead of This | Say This |
|----------------|----------|
| "This is a 16 SEER unit" | "This is our high-efficiency model — most homeowners see their electric bill drop $40-60 per month" |
| "We use PEX piping" | "The piping we use is flexible and won't corrode — it'll outlast this house" |
| "Architectural shingles" | "These shingles are rated for 130 mph winds and come with a 30-year warranty" |
| "200 amp panel" | "This gives you enough power to add an EV charger, hot tub, or workshop without any issues" |

### Always Present Options (Never Single-Option Quotes)

A single-number quote turns the conversation into yes/no. Three options turn it into "which one?" This is the foundation of the Good-Better-Best framework (Section 5).

The psychology: When given one option, the customer's only decision is whether to buy or not. When given three, they're choosing between options — and "not buying" becomes a less natural choice.

### The Person Who Asks Questions Controls the Conversation

If the customer is asking all the questions, they're in control. If you're asking the questions, you're guiding the conversation toward a sale. Great salespeople redirect with questions:

- Customer: "How much does this cost?"
- You: "Great question — before I give you a number, let me ask: are you looking for the most affordable fix, or do you want something that handles this permanently?"

### Speed to Lead

The first company to respond to a lead wins 78% of the time (InsideSales.com / Drift research). This means:

- Answer calls within 3 rings
- Return missed calls within 5 minutes
- Respond to web leads within 5 minutes
- Have after-hours systems in place (answering service, text auto-reply, voicemail callback commitment)

A mediocre salesperson who responds fast will outsell a great salesperson who responds slow.

### Never Badmouth Competitors

When a customer mentions a competitor, never trash them. It makes you look insecure and unprofessional. Instead:

- "They're a solid company." (Neutral acknowledgment)
- "What I can tell you is what makes us different..." (Redirect to your strengths)
- "When you compare quotes, here's what to look for..." (Educate — positions you as the expert)

## 3. Phone Intake Scripts (Incoming Calls)

The incoming call is the most critical touchpoint in any local service business. A blown phone call means a blown lead — and that lead cost $50-300 to generate. These scripts are designed to convert callers into booked appointments.

### General Phone Rules

1. Answer within 3 rings. Every ring past 3 costs you conversions.
2. Smile when you answer — it changes your voice tone. Sounds ridiculous. Works.
3. Use the caller's name at least twice during the call.
4. Stand up when you take sales calls — it projects energy and confidence.
5. Never put a customer on hold for more than 30 seconds without checking back in.
6. End every call with a confirmed next step (appointment, callback, email).

---

### Script A: New Customer Call (First-Time Caller)

**[Phone rings — answer within 3 rings]**

**You:** "Thanks for calling [Business Name], this is [Your Name]. How can I help you today?"

**[Customer describes their issue]**

**You:** "Got it — I can definitely help with that. Let me ask a couple quick questions so we can send the right person out to you."

**Qualifying questions (pick the relevant ones):**

1. "What exactly is going on? When did you first notice it?"
2. "How long has this been happening?"
3. "Is this an emergency, or is it something we can schedule in the next day or two?"
4. "Has anyone else looked at it?"
5. "What's the address?"
6. "And what's the best number to reach you at?"

**[After qualifying]**

**You:** "Great, I've got [Tech Name] available [day/time]. He's one of our best — been doing this [X] years. He'll come out, take a look at everything, and walk you through exactly what's going on and what your options are. Sound good?"

**[If they say yes]**

**You:** "Perfect. You'll get a text confirmation with [Tech Name]'s photo and arrival window so you know exactly who's coming. Is there anything else I can help with before we get you on the schedule?"

**[If they hesitate about the appointment]**

**You:** "No pressure at all. What would make you more comfortable — would it help if I had [Tech Name] give you a quick call first to talk through what you're dealing with?"

---

### Script B: Price Shopper (They Lead with "How Much Does It Cost?")

Price shoppers are not bad leads. They're just uninformed buyers who don't know what else to ask. Your job is to educate them without being condescending.

**Customer:** "Yeah, I just need a price on [service]."

**You:** "Great question on pricing. Every job is a little different depending on what we find when we get there — things like the age of your system, your home's layout, and what's actually causing the issue all affect the price."

**You:** "What I can tell you is our diagnostic fee is [$X], and that gets applied toward any work we do — so if you go ahead with the repair, the diagnostic is basically free. Most [service type] jobs run between [$low] and [$high] depending on what's needed."

**You:** "The best thing to do is have one of our guys come out and give you an exact number. That way you're not guessing. Can I get someone out to take a look?"

**[If they push harder on price]**

**You:** "I totally understand wanting a number upfront. Here's the thing — if I give you a number over the phone without seeing it, it's either going to be too high and scare you off, or too low and I'd have to change it when we get there. Neither one of those is fair to you. Let me send someone out so you get an accurate number you can trust."

**[If they say they're calling around]**

**You:** "Smart — I'd do the same thing. When you're comparing, make sure you ask each company three things: Are they licensed and insured? Do they guarantee their work in writing? And will they show you photos of what they find? That'll help you compare apples to apples."

---

### Script C: After-Hours Voicemail

**Voicemail greeting:**

"Hi, you've reached [Business Name]. We're helping other customers right now, but your call is really important to us. Leave your name, number, and a quick description of what's going on, and we'll call you back within [X] minutes."

"For after-hours emergencies, text us at [number] — we monitor texts around the clock."

"Thanks for calling [Business Name] — we'll talk to you soon."

**Key points:**
- "Helping other customers" is better than "closed" — it implies you're busy and in-demand
- Commit to a specific callback time and honor it
- Text option captures leads who won't leave voicemails (increasingly common, especially under-40 demographic)

---

### Script D: Returning a Missed Call

**You:** "Hi, this is [Name] from [Business Name] — I'm returning your call from [time]. Sorry I missed you. What can I help you with?"

**[If voicemail — leave this message]**

"Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] from [Business Name] returning your call. I want to make sure we get you taken care of. You can reach me directly at [number], or just text me and I'll get right back to you. Talk soon."

**Key: Call back within 5 minutes.** Set up missed-call alerts on your phone. Every minute you wait, the chance of booking drops.

---

### Script E: Web Lead / Form Submission Follow-Up

**[Call within 5 minutes of form submission]**

**You:** "Hi [Name], this is [Your Name] from [Business Name]. You just filled out a request on our website about [service]. I wanted to call you right away — what's going on?"

**Why this works:** Immediacy creates a "wow" factor. They just hit submit and the phone rings — that signals professionalism, urgency, and competence. It also catches them while the problem is top-of-mind.

## 4. In-Home / On-Site Sales Presentation

This is the full talk track for when you're face-to-face with the customer, whether it's a technician running a service call, an owner doing an estimate, or a dedicated sales rep doing an in-home consultation.

### Step 1: Arrival (First 2 Minutes Set the Tone)

The first 2 minutes determine whether the customer trusts you. You are being evaluated the moment you pull into the driveway.

**Before you knock:**
- Park on the street (not the driveway) unless they tell you otherwise
- Put on shoe covers or booties before entering
- Have your ID badge visible
- Carry a clipboard or tablet — it signals professionalism

**At the door:**

**You:** "Hi, I'm [Name] from [Business Name]. Thanks for having us out today. Before I take a look at anything, mind if I ask you a couple questions about what's been going on?"

**Why "before I take a look":** It signals that you care about their experience, not just the equipment. It slows things down and builds rapport. Jumping straight to the equipment makes you look like every other contractor.

### Step 2: Discovery (5-10 Minutes)

This is where you earn the sale. The more they talk, the more committed they become.

**Core discovery questions:**

1. "When did you first notice the issue?"
2. "Has it been getting worse, or is it pretty consistent?"
3. "Has anyone else looked at it? What did they say?"
4. "On a scale of 1-10, how urgent is this for you right now?"
5. "How long have you been in this home?"
6. "Is there anything else while I'm here that you'd like me to check out?"
7. "If we find something that needs attention, are you the decision-maker, or is there someone else we should loop in?"

**Question 7 is critical.** If the spouse needs to be involved, find out NOW — not after you've done the full presentation. Options:
- "Would it make sense to have them join us for the options review? Even by phone works great."
- If spouse isn't available, present anyway but set expectations: "I'll walk you through everything so you can share it with [spouse]. I'll also leave you a written proposal."

**Active listening techniques during discovery:**
- Repeat back what they said: "So it sounds like it started about two weeks ago and it's gotten louder since then — is that right?"
- Take notes visibly. It shows you care about the details.
- Nod. Make eye contact. Don't check your phone.

### Step 3: Inspection (10-30 Minutes Depending on Trade)

This is where most contractors go silent. They disappear into the crawlspace or onto the roof and come back with a number. That's a mistake. The inspection is a selling opportunity.

**Narrate what you're doing:**

"I'm going to check your [component] now. This is typically where we see issues in homes this age."

"Okay, I can see [finding]. Let me show you what I mean..."

**Take photos and show the customer:**
- Use your phone or tablet to photograph issues
- Show them what you're seeing: "See this right here? That's [issue]. What that means for you is [consequence]."
- Photos build trust — you're being transparent, not making things up

**Document everything:**
- Even things that are fine: "Your [component] is in great shape — no issues there."
- This builds credibility. If you only point out problems, they wonder if you're manufacturing them.

### Step 4: Transition to Presentation

**You:** "Alright, I've finished my inspection. I found a few things I want to walk you through. Can we sit down at the kitchen table for a few minutes? I want to show you what I found and give you some options."

**Why the kitchen table:** It's comfortable, it's where families make decisions, and it puts you at eye level (not standing over them). If they suggest staying in the garage or at the equipment, gently redirect: "It might be easier to spread out at a table — I've got some photos and options to show you."

### Step 5: Good-Better-Best Presentation (See Section 5 for Full Framework)

**Transition language:**

**You:** "Based on what I found, I want to give you three options so you can pick what works best for your budget and your situation. I always like to give choices because every homeowner's priorities are different."

Present the three tiers. See Section 5 for the complete GBB framework, tier design by trade, pricing psychology, and presentation scripts.

### Step 6: Ask for the Sale

After presenting all three options:

**You:** "So those are your three options. Which one feels right for you?"

**Then be silent.** This is the hardest part. Do not fill the silence. Do not add qualifiers. Do not say "no pressure." Just wait.

The first person who talks after the close loses negotiating position. Let them process. If it takes 30 seconds of silence, that's fine. They're thinking. Let them think.

**If they choose an option:** Confirm it, express genuine enthusiasm (not relief), and move to scheduling.

**If they have questions:** Answer them, then ask again: "Does that clear things up? Which option works best?"

**If they raise an objection:** See Section 6 for objection handling.

## 5. Good-Better-Best (GBB) Pricing Presentation

This is the single highest-impact change a local service business can make to increase revenue. Moving from single-option quotes to a three-tier GBB presentation increases average ticket size by 15-25% across all trades.

### Why GBB Works

- **62% of high-profit contractors use GBB** (Service Titan industry data)
- **Most customers choose Better** (the middle option) — this is called the "center-stage effect" or "Goldilocks principle"
- **Good anchors low** — it shows you're not trying to oversell anyone
- **Best anchors high** — it makes Better look reasonable by comparison
- **Choice = control** — customers feel empowered, not pressured
- **It eliminates "I need to get other quotes"** — you already gave them three options with different price points

### The Three Tiers

| Tier | What It Is | Pricing Strategy | Purpose |
|------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| **Good** | Minimum to solve the problem safely and correctly. Code-compliant, basic materials, standard warranty. | Anchor low. This should be your "we'll do it right, just without the extras" option. | Shows you're not trying to oversell. Gives budget-conscious customers a dignified option. |
| **Better** | Recommended solution. Better equipment/materials, longer warranty, added value items. | Target tier — this is where you want 50%+ of sales. Price 20-30% above Good. | The "sweet spot." When customers see Good is bare-bones and Best is premium, Better feels like the smart choice. |
| **Best** | Premium everything. Top-tier equipment, maximum warranty, included extras, white-glove service. | Aspirational. Price 40-60% above Good. | Makes Better look reasonable. Some customers will choose it and you'll have high-margin work. |

### Price Spacing Psychology

The gap between tiers matters. If they're too close, there's no reason to pick Good. If they're too far apart, Best feels absurd.

**Recommended spacing:**
- Good to Better: 20-30% increase
- Good to Best: 40-60% increase
- Example: If Good is $5,000, Better should be $6,000-6,500, Best should be $7,000-8,000

**The "decoy" effect:** Best makes Better look like a deal. This is deliberate. You WANT people to look at Best, feel it's a stretch, and feel smart choosing Better. That's the design working.

### Presentation Order

**Always present Good first.** Here's why:
1. It establishes that you're not pushy — you're showing the basic option upfront
2. Each subsequent tier adds value — the conversation builds momentum
3. By the time you get to Best, they've mentally been climbing a ladder of "wouldn't it be nice if..."

**Some sales trainers say present Best first.** For local service businesses, this backfires. Homeowners get sticker shock, shut down emotionally, and then Good feels like a consolation prize. Start low, build up.

### Presentation Script Template

**You:** "Based on everything I found today, I've put together three options for you. Let me walk you through each one."

**Good:**
"Option one is what I call the Good option. This takes care of the [core issue] — it gets you back up and running safely and everything is done to code. It includes [scope]. The investment for this option is [$X]."

"This is a solid option if your main priority right now is just solving the immediate problem."

**Better:**
"Option two is what I recommend for most of my customers — I call it the Better option. This includes everything in the Good option, plus [additional scope]. You also get [warranty upgrade / added value]. The investment for this is [$Y]."

"The reason I recommend this one is [specific benefit tied to their situation — e.g., 'given the age of your system, the extended warranty really pays for itself' or 'since you mentioned your energy bills have been high, this efficiency upgrade typically saves $40-60 a month']."

**Best:**
"Option three is the Best option — this is the premium package. It includes everything in the Better option, plus [premium additions]. You get [maximum warranty / extras / premium materials]. The investment for this is [$Z]."

"This is for homeowners who want to do it once, do it right, and not think about it again for [X] years."

**Close:**
"So those are your three options. Which one feels right for you and your family?"

**[Silence — wait for response]**

### Financing Integration

For Better and Best tiers on jobs over $2,000, ALWAYS present a monthly payment option alongside the total price.

**Script:** "The total for the Better option is [$Y], and if you'd prefer to spread that out, it comes to about [$monthly] a month with financing. A lot of my customers go that route — same great work, just easier on the monthly budget."

**Key:** Present financing as normal and smart, never as a last resort. Don't say "if you can't afford it..." Say "if you'd prefer to spread it out..."

## 6. Objection Handling

Objections are not rejections. They are requests for more information, reassurance, or a different approach. Every objection is an opportunity to deepen the conversation.

**Core principle:** Never argue with an objection. Acknowledge it, ask a clarifying question, then redirect.

The formula: **Acknowledge > Clarify > Redirect**

### Objection 1: "It's Too Expensive" / "That's More Than I Expected"

**Psychology:** This is almost never about the actual dollar amount. It's about perceived value relative to price, or it's a budget/cash flow issue. Your job is to find out which one.

**Script:**

**You:** "I totally hear you — it's a big investment. When you say it's expensive, do you mean it's not worth it, or it's just more than you planned to spend right now?"

**If it's a budget issue (cash flow):**
"If we could make it fit your budget with a monthly payment, would you want to fix it the right way? ... Our financing is [terms] — that brings the Better option down to about [$X/month]. That's less than most people's cable bill, and you get [benefit]."

**If it's a value issue (they don't see the worth):**
"What would you need to see to feel confident this is the right solution for your home?"
Listen, then re-anchor on outcomes: "Here's what you're really getting — [comfort/safety/savings/peace of mind]. And with the [warranty], if anything goes wrong in the next [X] years, we come back and fix it at no cost."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Well, quality isn't cheap." (Condescending)
- "You get what you pay for." (Cliche, dismissive)
- "Let me see what I can do on the price." (Instantly devalues your work)

### Objection 2: "I Need to Talk to My Spouse"

**Psychology:** Sometimes genuine. Often a polite way to exit without saying no. Your job is to determine which one and help them get to a decision.

**Script:**

**You:** "Totally understand — that's a big decision to make together. What are the top two questions you already know they're going to ask?"

**[Answer those questions right there on the spot.]**

**You:** "Now that we've covered those — if we answer them now, would you be comfortable giving [spouse] a quick two-minute call so we can all decide together? I can step outside and give you privacy."

**If they won't call:**

**You:** "No problem at all. I'll leave you a written proposal with all three options, the photos of what I found, and my direct number. When you and [spouse] talk tonight, feel free to call or text me with any questions — even after hours, I'll respond."

**What NOT to say:**
- "Can I call your spouse right now?" (Aggressive, presumptuous)
- "Your spouse will probably agree." (Dismissive of their partnership)

### Objection 3: "I'm Going to Get Other Quotes"

**Psychology:** They want validation that your price is fair. This is actually a buying signal — they're serious enough to shop. Your job is to position yourself as the standard they compare others against.

**Script:**

**You:** "Smart move — I'd do the same thing. When you're comparing quotes, make sure you compare three things: (1) Is the system properly sized for your home? Undersized systems fail early. (2) What's included in the install — are they pulling permits, doing a load calculation, cleaning up after? (3) What happens after the sale — warranty, response time, who do you call if there's an issue at 10 PM?"

**Pause, then:**

**You:** "The cheapest system installed wrong is the most expensive system you can buy. I'd rather you pick the company that does it right — whether that's us or someone else."

**Why this works:** You've just educated them. Now every other contractor who shows up and doesn't address those three things looks less professional. You've set the criteria in your favor without badmouthing anyone.

**What NOT to say:**
- "We'll match any quote." (Race to the bottom)
- "Who else are you talking to?" (Sounds insecure)

### Objection 4: "I Need to Think About It"

**Psychology:** "Think about it" means there's an unresolved concern they haven't voiced. Your job is to surface it.

**Script:**

**You:** "Of course — take all the time you need. Usually when someone says that, it means one of two things: they're unsure about the money, or they're unsure about the decision itself. Which one is it for you?"

**If money:** Pivot to financing. "Would a monthly payment option make this easier?"

**If decision:** "What part feels unclear or risky right now?" Then address whatever they surface.

**If they truly want time:**

**You:** "Totally fair. I'll leave you a written proposal. I'll check in with you tomorrow — not to pressure you, just to see if any questions came up. What's the best time to call?"

**What NOT to say:**
- "What's there to think about?" (Confrontational)
- "This price is only good today." (False urgency — destroys trust)

### Objection 5: "Can You Do Better on Price?"

**Psychology:** They're testing you. This is a negotiation reflex, not necessarily a dealbreaker. If you fold immediately, they'll wonder what else you were overcharging on.

**Script:**

**You:** "Are you trying to lower the monthly payment, the total price, or are you still kind of feeling things out?"

**If monthly payment:** Pivot to financing options or longer terms.

**If total price:** "I can't lower the price — our pricing is based on doing the job correctly with quality materials and standing behind it. But what I could do is include [added value: extra maintenance visit, extended labor warranty, upgraded thermostat, etc.] at no extra charge."

**If feeling it out:** Go back to discovery. "What would make this feel like a no-brainer for you?"

**Golden rule: NEVER just discount.** Trade value instead. Discounting tells the customer your original price was inflated. Adding value tells them you're going the extra mile.

**What NOT to say:**
- "Sure, let me see what I can take off." (You just lost all pricing credibility)
- "That's already the best price." (Flat, unhelpful, conversation-ender)

## 7. Upsell Frameworks

Upselling is not about squeezing more money out of customers. It's about solving problems they didn't know they had, or offering convenience and protection they'll appreciate. Done right, customers thank you for the upsell.

### Upsell Type 1: Maintenance Agreement (At Job Completion)

**When to use:** After completing any service call or installation.

**Script:**

**You:** "Before I head out — we have a maintenance program I want to mention because it actually saves you money. It covers your annual tune-ups — one in spring, one in fall — and gives you priority scheduling, so you skip the wait during peak season. It also gives you [X]% off any future repairs."

"It's [$X/month], and honestly it pays for itself with the tune-ups alone — those run [$Y] each without the plan. Want me to set you up?"

**Why it works at job completion:** They just had a positive experience. They're grateful the problem is fixed. This is peak trust and satisfaction. Asking them to sign up for ongoing protection is natural, not salesy.

### Upsell Type 2: Add-On Service (During the Service Call)

**When to use:** When you discover a secondary issue during an inspection or repair.

**Script:**

**You:** "While I've got this open, I noticed [related issue]. It's not an emergency right now, but it's going to need attention in the next [timeframe]. If I fix it now while I'm here, it would be [$X]. If we have to come back for a separate visit, it'd be [$Y] — because of the trip charge and having to open everything back up."

"Want me to take care of it while I'm already here?"

**Why it works:** Convenience + cost savings. Fixing it now saves them a second disruption and a second trip charge. You're doing them a favor, not upselling.

**Key: Always present the "now vs. later" comparison.** The price differential makes the upsell feel like a deal, not an addition.

### Upsell Type 3: Upgrade (During the Estimate)

**When to use:** This is built into the GBB framework. The Better and Best tiers ARE the upsell.

The upgrade upsell happens naturally when you present options. You don't need a separate pitch — the GBB structure handles it.

**Additional language for upgrades:**

- "A lot of my customers in homes like yours go with the Better option because [specific reason tied to their home/situation]."
- "If it were my house, I'd go with the Better option — here's why..."
- "The difference between Good and Better is [$X], but you get [list of added value]. Most people find that's worth it."

### Upsell Type 4: Seasonal or Complementary Service

**When to use:** When doing work in one trade that relates to another service you offer.

**Examples:**
- HVAC install → "We also do duct cleaning — since we're putting in a brand new system, it makes sense to start with clean ducts. We can do it the same day for [$X]."
- Plumbing repair → "While I'm here, want me to do a quick water quality test? It's free and takes 2 minutes."
- Roofing → "We noticed your gutters are [issue]. We can include gutter replacement in the project for [$X] added."
- Electrical → "Since we're already in the panel, adding a whole-home surge protector is [$X]. It protects everything in the house."

## 8. Closing Techniques

Closing is not a trick. It's guiding the customer to a decision they already want to make. If you've done the discovery, presentation, and objection handling right, closing should feel natural.

### Technique 1: Assumptive Close

**What it is:** You assume the sale is happening and move straight to logistics.

**Script:** "Let me get you on the schedule. We've got [day] available — does morning or afternoon work better for you?"

**When to use:** After they've indicated strong interest, nodded along during the presentation, or said something like "that sounds good" or "I like that option."

### Technique 2: Alternative Close

**What it is:** You give them a choice between two positive options (not between yes and no).

**Script:** "Would you like to go with the Better package, or does the Best make more sense for your situation?"

**When to use:** When they're stuck between options. This removes "no" from the equation.

### Technique 3: Urgency Close (ONLY When Genuine)

**What it is:** You create time-based motivation. But ONLY when the urgency is real. Fake urgency destroys trust instantly.

**Legitimate urgency examples:**
- "I can hold this price through [date], but material costs are going up [X]% next month." (Only if true)
- "Our schedule is filling up fast for [season] — if we book today, I can get you in [date]. If we wait, it might be [later date]."
- "The rebate from the manufacturer expires [date]."
- "This is a safety issue — I'd hate for [consequence] to happen while you're waiting."

**Script:** "I want to be upfront — this pricing is based on current material costs, and we've been notified of a [X]% increase coming [date]. I can lock in today's price if we get you on the schedule."

**What NOT to do:** "This price is only good today." (Unless it genuinely is — and it almost never is. Customers know this is a tactic and it erodes trust.)

### Technique 4: Summary Close

**What it is:** You recap everything in a neat package and ask for confirmation.

**Script:** "So here's what we're going to do — [scope of work]. It comes with [warranty]. We can start [date]. And with financing, it's [$X/month]. Does that all sound good?"

**When to use:** When you've addressed all objections and the customer seems ready but hasn't pulled the trigger. The summary gives them a final mental picture of what they're getting.

### Technique 5: The "Feel Right" Close

**What it is:** An emotionally-oriented close that respects their decision-making process.

**Script:** "Which option feels right for you and your family?"

**When to use:** After the GBB presentation. "Feels right" is deliberately soft — it's not "which one do you want to buy" (transactional) or "which one are you going with" (pushy). It invites them to make a values-based decision.

### Technique 6: The Takeaway Close

**What it is:** You subtly reduce options to create a fear of missing out.

**Script:** "Honestly, for your situation, you probably don't need the Best option. The Better package handles everything you're dealing with. Want to go with that?"

**Why it works:** By taking away the premium option, you make them feel like they're getting an honest recommendation, not a hard sell. Paradoxically, some customers will insist on Best because you told them they didn't need it.

## 9. Follow-Up After the Estimate

Follow-up is where most local service businesses lose deals they already earned. The customer was interested, the presentation was good, but nobody called them back and they went with whoever followed up.

**For the full follow-up sequence, drip campaign, and automated tracking, see the `estimate-tracker` skill.**

Here are the critical follow-up scripts:

### Same Day Follow-Up (2-4 Hours After the Appointment)

**You (call or text):** "Hi [Name], this is [Tech/Name] from [Business]. I just wanted to make sure you didn't have any questions about the options we went over today. I also wanted to thank you for your time — I know having someone out to the house takes time out of your day."

**Purpose:** This is not a pressure call. It's a service call. It reinforces that you care and keeps the door open.

### Day 2 Follow-Up

**You (text is usually better):** "Hi [Name], checking in on your [service] decision. Happy to answer any questions you or [spouse] might have. Our schedule is filling up for [timeframe] — just wanted to give you a heads up so we can save your spot."

### Day 5 Follow-Up (If No Response)

**You:** "Hi [Name], I wanted to reach out one more time about the [service] options we discussed. I know these decisions take time. If you've decided to go a different direction, no hard feelings at all — I just want to make sure you have everything you need to make the best choice for your family. Call or text anytime."

**Key:** The day-5 message gives them an exit. "No hard feelings" takes the pressure off and paradoxically makes them more likely to respond. People avoid calls from pushy salespeople.

## 10. Sales Roleplay / Practice Mode

When the owner says "let's practice," "roleplay," "I want to run through a scenario," or "can we practice selling":

### How Roleplay Works

1. **The AI plays the homeowner.** The AI will present a realistic scenario with a backstory, objections, personality, and hidden motivations.
2. **The owner/salesperson practices their pitch.** They handle the discovery, presentation, objections, and close.
3. **The AI responds realistically.** Not as a pushover, and not as an impossible customer — as a realistic homeowner with genuine concerns.
4. **After the roleplay, the AI gives structured feedback.**

### Roleplay Scenarios (AI Will Select or the Owner Can Request)

**Scenario 1: The Price Shopper**
"I'm a homeowner in a 2,500 sq ft house built in 1998. My AC stopped working yesterday. I already got one quote for $8,500 and I think that's insane. I'm calling around."

**Scenario 2: The Spouse Excuse**
"I'm interested in getting my roof replaced — it's 22 years old and starting to leak. But I can't make any big decisions without my husband, and he's out of town until Friday."

**Scenario 3: The DIY Skeptic**
"I watched a YouTube video that says this is a $200 fix and any homeowner can do it. Why are you charging me $1,500?"

**Scenario 4: The Previous Bad Experience**
"The last plumber we hired was terrible — showed up late, left a mess, and charged us way more than the quote. I don't trust contractors."

**Scenario 5: The Tire Kicker**
"We're thinking about maybe replacing our furnace sometime in the next year or two. We're not in a rush. Just want to get some information."

**Scenario 6: The Everything-Is-Fine Denier**
"I called because my neighbor suggested I get a tune-up, but honestly everything seems to be working fine. I don't think anything is wrong."

### Feedback Framework (After Roleplay)

The AI will evaluate on these criteria:

1. **Discovery:** Did you ask enough questions? Did you listen?
2. **Rapport:** Did you build trust early? Did you make the customer feel heard?
3. **Presentation:** Did you present options (GBB)? Did you sell outcomes, not equipment?
4. **Objection handling:** Did you acknowledge, clarify, and redirect? Or did you argue?
5. **Closing:** Did you ask for the sale? Did you use appropriate technique?
6. **Overall:** What was the strongest moment? What would you change?

The AI will provide specific phrases the salesperson could have used and offer to run the same scenario again to practice the improvements.

## 11. Metrics to Track

If you're not measuring, you're guessing. These are the critical sales metrics every local service business should track.

### Primary Metrics

| Metric | Target | How to Calculate |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| **Close Rate** | 40-60% (residential service) | Deals won / Total estimates given |
| **Average Ticket Size** | Varies by trade, but should trend UP | Total revenue / Number of jobs |
| **GBB Tier Distribution** | 20% Good, 50% Better, 30% Best | Track which tier each customer selects |
| **Lead Response Time** | Under 5 minutes | Time from lead to first contact |
| **Estimate Follow-Up Rate** | 100% of unsold estimates get follow-up | Followed-up / Total unsold |

### Secondary Metrics

| Metric | Target | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| **Booking Rate** (calls to appointments) | 75-85% | Measures phone script effectiveness |
| **Maintenance Agreement Conversion** | 30-40% of completed jobs | Recurring revenue + retention |
| **Financing Utilization** | 25-35% of jobs over $2K | If it's low, the team isn't offering it |
| **Average Options Presented** | 3 per estimate | If it's 1, they're not doing GBB |
| **Revenue per Lead** | Track monthly | Combines close rate + ticket size into one number |

### GBB Tier Distribution Analysis

The ideal distribution tells you a lot:

| Distribution | What It Means |
|-------------|---------------|
| 70%+ Good | Team is not presenting Better/Best effectively. Value selling needs work. |
| 60%+ Better | Perfect. The system is working as designed. |
| 40%+ Best | Either you're underpricing Good/Better, or your team is excellent at value selling. |
| Even split (33/33/33) | Good pricing strategy, effective presentation. |
| 80%+ any single tier | Something is off — pricing or presentation needs adjustment. |

### Tracking Implementation

Recommend that the owner track these in whatever system they use (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Jobber, spreadsheet, etc.). If they don't have a system:

- Start with a simple spreadsheet: Date, Customer, Service, Options Presented, Option Chosen, Total, Close/Lost, Reason if Lost
- Review weekly — just 15 minutes to spot trends
- Share results with the team — what gets measured gets managed

## 12. Sales Culture & Team Training

For businesses with multiple salespeople or technicians who sell:

### Weekly Sales Huddles (15 Minutes)

- Review last week's close rate and average ticket
- Share one win: what worked on a specific call
- Share one loss: what went wrong, how to handle it next time
- Practice one objection as a group (roleplay format)
- Set one goal for the week (e.g., "present GBB on every call" or "always offer financing")

### Ride-Alongs

- New salespeople should ride along with the best closer for at least 5 calls before going solo
- After 5 ride-alongs, reverse it — they lead, the experienced person observes and gives feedback
- Monthly ride-alongs even for experienced reps keep skills sharp

### Compensation Alignment

Sales behaviors follow compensation. If you want GBB presentations, reward average ticket size, not just close rate. If you want maintenance agreements sold, pay a bonus per agreement.
