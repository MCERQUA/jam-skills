## Why GBB Works

### The Data

- **62% of high-profit contractors** use some form of Good-Better-Best (Service Titan industry reports)
- **Average ticket increase of 15-25%** when switching from single-option quotes to GBB
- **Close rates improve 10-15%** because customers choose between options instead of yes/no
- **Most customers choose Better (the middle tier)** — the center-stage effect / Goldilocks principle
- Companies using GBB report **fewer price objections** because customers see the range and self-select

### The Psychology

**Anchoring:** The Good option sets a low anchor. The Best option sets a high anchor. Better sits comfortably in the middle and feels like the smart choice.

**Choice architecture:** One option creates a buy/don't-buy decision. Three options create a which-one decision. The mental frame shifts from "should I?" to "which should I?"

**Loss aversion:** When customers see what they'd miss by choosing Good (warranty, better equipment, added value), they feel the loss of those benefits. Better captures most of those benefits at a moderate premium.

**Decoy effect:** Best makes Better look like a deal. If Better is $6,500 and Best is $9,000, Better feels very reasonable. Without Best on the page, $6,500 might feel expensive on its own.

## Price Spacing Psychology

The gap between tiers is not arbitrary. It's engineered to drive behavior.

### Recommended Spacing

| Tier | Price Relationship | Example (if Good = $5,000) |
|------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| Good | Baseline | $5,000 |
| Better | 20-30% above Good | $6,000 - $6,500 |
| Best | 40-60% above Good | $7,000 - $8,000 |

### Why These Numbers

**Good to Better (20-30% gap):**
- Small enough that the upgrade feels accessible
- Most customers will mentally justify the difference: "For just $1,000-1,500 more, I get..."
- If the gap is less than 15%, there's no reason to choose Good and it feels like a fake option
- If the gap is more than 35%, the jump feels too big and people stay with Good

**Better to Best (additional 20-30% above Better):**
- Large enough that Best feels genuinely premium
- Small enough that some customers will stretch for it
- If Best is too close to Better, everyone picks Best and your "luxury" tier becomes your standard
- If Best is too far from Better, nobody picks Best and it's a wasted option

### The "No Dead Zones" Rule

Every tier should have buyers. If after 20+ presentations:
- Nobody picks Good: It's priced too close to Better, or Good is embarrassingly bare-bones. Add more to Good or widen the gap.
- Nobody picks Better: The value add over Good isn't clear enough. Enhance Better's differentiators.
- Nobody picks Best: It's overpriced or the extras aren't compelling. Either reduce Best price or add more desirable items.
- Everyone picks one tier: Your spacing or value differentiation needs adjustment.

### Target Distribution

| Tier | Ideal Distribution | Revenue Impact |
|------|-------------------|----------------|
| Good | 20% | Captures budget buyers who would otherwise walk |
| Better | 50% | Your highest-volume tier with healthy margins |
| Best | 30% | Premium margin, loyal customers, referral generators |

## Tier Design Principles

### Good Tier Rules

1. **Must fully solve the problem.** Good is not a half-measure. It's code-compliant, safe, and correct. The customer should never regret choosing Good.
2. **Use standard-grade materials.** Not cheap — standard. There's a difference.
3. **Include standard warranty.** Manufacturer default or your company's base warranty.
4. **Don't pad it with unnecessary items.** Keep it clean and honest.
5. **It must be something you'd be proud to install.** If you wouldn't put your name on it, don't offer it.

### Better Tier Rules

1. **This is your recommended option.** Design it as the one YOU would choose for your own home.
2. **Upgrade equipment one tier.** Better brand, better efficiency, better performance.
3. **Extended warranty.** 5-year → 10-year, parts → parts + labor, etc.
4. **Include 1-2 value adds** that don't cost you much but have high perceived value. Examples: smart thermostat ($150 cost, $300 perceived value), free first-year maintenance ($200 cost, $400 perceived value), upgraded filter system.
5. **Explain WHY it's better in customer language.** Not specs — outcomes.

### Best Tier Rules

1. **Premium everything.** Top-tier equipment, best materials, maximum warranty.
2. **Include "luxury" items** that make the customer feel special. White-glove service, post-install walk-through, extended support.
3. **Add items that create long-term relationship.** Multi-year maintenance plan, priority service, annual inspections.
4. **Best should feel aspirational but attainable.** Not absurdly expensive — just the "if money weren't an issue" option.
5. **Some customers WILL choose Best.** Don't make it a throwaway. Make it genuinely excellent.

### Cross-Tier Rules

- **Every tier must be clearly different.** The customer should immediately understand what changes between tiers.
- **Use the same format for all three.** Side-by-side comparison. Same categories, different items.
- **Include warranty, timeline, and support in every tier.** These are decision factors, not afterthoughts.
- **Never make Good look bad to sell Better.** Good should look honest and decent. Better should look smart. Best should look impressive.

## Visual Presentation Tips

How you present GBB matters as much as what you present. A verbal walk-through is easily forgotten. A visual side-by-side comparison is a decision tool.

### Format Options

**Option 1: Printed Leave-Behind (Best for in-home sales)**
- One page, landscape orientation
- Three columns (Good | Better | Best)
- Checkmarks for included items, dashes for excluded
- Better column highlighted or bordered
- Price at the bottom of each column
- Monthly payment shown below total price for Better and Best
- Company logo and tech/salesperson name at top

**Option 2: Tablet / iPad Presentation**
- Same layout as printed, but interactive
- Tap to expand details on any line item
- Photos of actual equipment/materials
- Links to manufacturer spec sheets if customer wants to dive deeper
- Digital signature capture if they choose on the spot

**Option 3: Whiteboard / Paper (Field Techs)**
- Draw three columns on the customer's kitchen table
- Quick and authentic — doesn't feel rehearsed
- Write key items and prices
- Circle the recommended option
- Leave the paper with them

### Design Rules

1. **Better should visually stand out.** Use a highlight color, a "RECOMMENDED" badge, or a slightly larger column. The eye should be drawn to it first.
2. **Use checkmarks and X marks.** Visual shorthand is faster than reading descriptions. Customer can instantly see what they get and don't get in each tier.
3. **Group items into categories.** Equipment, Installation, Warranty, Extras. Don't just list randomly.
4. **Show the price LAST.** Walk through value before revealing cost. On the printed version, the price is at the bottom — they have to see everything included before they get to the number.
5. **Include a photo of the equipment** (or similar completed project for roofing/landscaping). Visual = real. Text = abstract.

### Presentation Body Language

- Sit next to the customer (side by side), not across from them. You're on the same team, looking at the same document.
- Point to items as you discuss them. Physically engaging with the document keeps attention.
- When presenting Better, lean in slightly and slow down. You're spending more time here because it's your recommendation.
- When presenting Best, smile and use phrases like "This is the dream scenario" or "If money were no object, this is what I'd put in my own home."

## Financing Integration

Financing is not a backup plan for customers who can't afford it. It's a strategic tool that should be part of every Better and Best presentation.

### When to Present Financing

- **Always** on jobs over $2,000
- **Always** as part of the Better and Best tier presentation (not as a separate conversation)
- **Never** as "if you can't afford it" — always as "if you'd prefer to spread it out"

### How to Present It

**Script (during Better presentation):**

"The total for the Better option is $7,500. If you'd prefer to spread that out, it comes to about $125 a month with our 0% financing for 60 months. A lot of my customers go that route — same great work, just easier on the monthly budget."

**Script (during Best presentation):**

"The Best option is $12,000, which comes to about $200 a month with financing. When you think about it, that's about $6.50 a day for [benefit: a brand new roof that lasts 50 years / a comfort system that cuts your energy bill in half]."

### Monthly Payment Comparisons

Make the monthly payment relatable:

| Monthly Payment | Comparison |
|----------------|-----------|
| $75/month | "Less than your streaming subscriptions" |
| $100/month | "About what most people spend eating out" |
| $150/month | "Less than a car payment" |
| $200/month | "About $6.50 a day — less than a coffee and a sandwich" |

### Financing Tips

1. **Pre-qualify before the visit if possible.** Knowing they're approved removes a barrier.
2. **Have the application ready.** Don't make them go home and apply — do it on the spot.
3. **Offer multiple terms.** 36 months (lower total), 60 months (lower payment), 84 months (lowest payment).
4. **0% financing is a powerful closer.** "Same as cash" makes people feel smart, not stretched.
5. **Never say "credit check."** Say "quick application" or "pre-qualification." Credit check sounds scary.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Making Good Too Bad

If Good is a bare-bones, embarrassing option, customers feel manipulated. They think: "You're obviously trying to force me into the expensive one." Good should be a legitimate solution you'd stand behind.

**Fix:** Make Good a real option. You'd install it at your mom's house and feel fine about it.

### Mistake 2: Making Better and Best Too Similar

If the only difference between Better and Best is a slightly longer warranty and $500, there's no reason to pick Best and Better loses its "middle ground" psychology.

**Fix:** Best needs genuinely different/premium items. Different equipment tier, different materials, additional services. The customer should look at Best and think "wow, that's the full treatment."

### Mistake 3: Not Explaining WHY Each Tier Exists

Listing items without context is a menu, not a presentation. The customer doesn't know what SEER ratings mean or why PEX is better than copper.

**Fix:** For every item, add one sentence of "what this means for you." Translate features into outcomes.

### Mistake 4: Presenting Only Verbally

A verbal-only presentation is forgotten within an hour. The customer can't compare tiers after you leave, can't show their spouse, and can't remember what was in each option.

**Fix:** Always leave a written or printed comparison. Even a handwritten one on paper is better than nothing.

### Mistake 5: Skipping Financing

If you present a $12,000 Best option without mentioning $200/month financing, you've given them sticker shock for no reason. Most people think in monthly payments, not lump sums.

**Fix:** Always present the monthly payment alongside the total for Better and Best.

### Mistake 6: Apologizing for Best

Some techs present Best sheepishly: "This one's pretty expensive, but..." That's sabotage. If you don't believe Best is worth the money, neither will the customer.

**Fix:** Present Best with confidence and enthusiasm. "This is the premium option — it's for homeowners who want to do it once, do it right, and not think about it again."

### Mistake 7: Not Anchoring to Time

A $10,000 roof sounds expensive. A $10,000 roof that lasts 50 years is $200/year or $0.55/day. Always break large numbers into smaller timeframes.

**Fix:** "Over the 30-year life of this roof, that's less than a dollar a day for complete protection."

### Mistake 8: Inconsistent Presentation

If every tech presents differently — different tiers, different pricing, different style — customers who get multiple estimates from the same company will notice. It also makes training impossible.

**Fix:** Standardize the GBB template for each service. Same tiers, same format, same language. Individual personality can vary, but the structure should be consistent.

### Mistake 9: Not Tracking Tier Selection

If you don't know your Good/Better/Best distribution, you can't optimize. Maybe your Better needs more value. Maybe your pricing is off. You'll never know without data.

**Fix:** Track every proposal: which tier was chosen (or if they didn't buy), and review monthly.

### Mistake 10: Offering More Than Three Options

Four or five tiers cause decision paralysis. The customer can't evaluate that many options and defaults to "I need to think about it." Three is the magic number — enough to feel like choice, not enough to overwhelm.

**Fix:** Stick to exactly three. If you have add-ons, present them separately after the tier choice is made.

## Example Presentation Scripts by Trade

### HVAC — Full Presentation Script

**Context:** Tech has completed inspection, found a failing 15-year-old AC in a 2,500 sq ft home. Homeowner is at the kitchen table.

**You:** "Alright, so here's what I found. Your AC is original to the home — it's about 15 years old. The compressor is starting to fail, and the refrigerant type it uses, R-22, was actually discontinued. So even if we repair it, finding refrigerant gets harder and more expensive every year."

"I've put together three options so you can decide what makes the most sense for your situation and your budget."

**[Place printed comparison on table, sit beside customer]**

"Option one — the Good option. We replace your AC with a brand new 14 SEER2 system. It's efficient, reliable, and it comes with a standard 5-year parts warranty. We include a programmable thermostat, full installation, pulling the permit, and hauling away the old unit. The investment for this is $5,800."

"This gets you cool air reliably for the next 10-15 years. If your main priority is solving the problem at the best price, this is a solid choice."

**[Pause — let them absorb]**

"Option two — the Better option. This is what I'd recommend, and what I'd put in my own house. We go with a 16 SEER2 two-stage system. Two-stage means it runs at a lower speed most of the time, which is quieter, more efficient, and keeps your home at a more even temperature. No hot and cold spots."

"You get a smart thermostat — you can control it from your phone, set schedules, and it learns your preferences. We also do a full duct inspection and seal any leaks, upgrade your air filter to a 4-inch media filter that catches allergens, and you get a 10-year parts warranty plus 3 years on labor."

"The best part — I'm including the first year of maintenance. We come out in spring and fall, tune everything up, catch any issues early. The investment for this is $7,400, or about $125 a month with financing."

"Most of my customers go with this one because the efficiency savings alone make up the difference over a few years."

**[Pause]**
