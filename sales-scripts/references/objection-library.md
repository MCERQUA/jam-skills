# Objection Library — Local Service Businesses

This is the expanded objection handling reference for the `sales-scripts` skill. It covers 11 common objections with the psychology behind each, what NOT to say, and 2-3 word-for-word response scripts per objection.

**Core formula for all objections: Acknowledge > Clarify > Redirect**

Never argue. Never get defensive. Every objection is a door, not a wall.

## 1. It's Too Expensive

### Psychology

"Too expensive" is almost never about the absolute dollar amount. It's one of three things:

- **Value gap:** They don't believe the solution is worth the price (perception problem)
- **Budget gap:** They want to fix it but don't have the money right now (cash flow problem)
- **Comparison gap:** They've seen a lower number somewhere and this doesn't match (framing problem)

Your job is to figure out which one it is. Each requires a completely different response.

### What NOT to Say

- "Well, quality isn't cheap." — Condescending. Makes them feel stupid for asking.
- "You get what you pay for." — Cliche. They've heard it from every contractor.
- "Let me see what I can do on the price." — Instantly signals your price was inflated. If you can lower it, why didn't you?
- "That's actually a really fair price." — Telling someone their feeling is wrong never works.

### Response Script A: The Diagnostic Question

**You:** "I hear you — it's a significant investment. When you say it's expensive, help me understand — do you feel like the price doesn't match the value, or is it more that it's just more than you planned to spend right now?"

**If value:** "What would you need to see to feel confident this is the right solution?" Listen, then re-anchor: "Here's what you're really getting — [outcome: comfort, safety, 15 years without thinking about it]. And with the [warranty], anything that goes wrong, we fix it. No extra charge."

**If budget:** "If we could break that into a monthly payment that fits your budget, would you want to go ahead and fix it the right way? Our financing brings this to about [$X/month] — less than [relatable comparison: a streaming subscription, a dinner out, a tank of gas]."

### Response Script B: The Reframe

**You:** "I totally understand. Let me ask you this — what would it cost you if this [fails / breaks down completely / causes water damage / creates a safety issue]? Most people don't think about the cost of NOT fixing it. A $6,000 repair now might save a $20,000 problem next winter."

### Response Script C: The Good Tier Redirect

**You:** "I want to make sure you have an option that works. The Good option I showed you handles the core problem at [$X]. Would that feel more comfortable? I always want to give you choices, not pressure."

## 2. I Need to Talk to My Spouse

### Psychology

This objection has two versions:

- **Genuine:** They actually need spousal buy-in for financial decisions. Respect this.
- **Exit strategy:** They're not sold but don't want to say no to your face. This is the polite rejection.

The way to tell the difference: genuine spouse objections come with specifics ("She'll want to know about the warranty" or "He's going to ask about financing"). Vague ones ("I just need to run it by them") are usually exit strategies.

### What NOT to Say

- "Can I talk to your spouse right now?" — Aggressive. You're not a used car dealer.
- "Your wife/husband will probably agree." — Presumptuous and dismissive of their partnership.
- "What's your spouse going to say no to?" — Confrontational.
- "I can wait while you call them." — Puts them on the spot.

### Response Script A: Surface the Real Questions

**You:** "Totally understand — big decisions should be made together. What are the top two questions you already know [spouse] is going to ask?"

**[They'll usually say price and whether it's really necessary]**

**You:** "Great questions. Let me answer those right now so when you talk tonight, you've already got the answers..."

**[Answer both questions thoroughly]**

**You:** "Now that we've covered those — would you be comfortable giving [spouse] a quick call so we can loop them in? Sometimes a two-minute conversation saves going back and forth. I'm happy to step outside."

### Response Script B: Equip Them to Sell For You

**You:** "Absolutely — I'll leave you a written proposal with all three options, the photos of what I found, and a side-by-side comparison. That way when you sit down with [spouse] tonight, you've got everything in front of you."

"I'll also include my direct cell number. If either of you have questions — even at 9 PM tonight — text me and I'll respond right away."

### Response Script C: Schedule the Follow-Up

**You:** "When do you think you'll be able to talk with [spouse]? ... Would it be okay if I called you [specific time] to see where you landed? That way if there are any questions, I can answer them while it's still fresh."

## 3. I'm Going to Get Other Quotes

### Psychology

This is actually a buying signal. They're serious enough to invest time shopping. They're not saying "no" — they're saying "convince me." Your job is to become the standard they measure everyone else against.

### What NOT to Say

- "We'll match any competitor's price." — Race to the bottom. You just told them your price is negotiable.
- "Who else are you talking to?" — Sounds insecure and competitive.
- "Other companies will lowball you." — Badmouthing competitors makes you look bad.
- "You won't find a better price." — Probably not true, and it's a dare.

### Response Script A: Set the Comparison Criteria

**You:** "Smart move — I'd do the same thing. When you're comparing, here are three things to look for so you're comparing apples to apples:"

"First — is the system properly sized for your home? A lot of companies skip the load calculation and just match what you have. That's how you end up with a system that's too big or too small."

"Second — what's included in the install? Permits, inspections, hauling away the old equipment, cleanup — some companies quote low and then those are all add-ons."

"Third — what happens after the sale? If something goes wrong at 10 PM on a Saturday, can you call someone? What does the warranty actually cover?"

**Pause, then:**

**You:** "The cheapest system installed wrong is the most expensive system you can buy. I'd rather you pick the company that does it right — whether that's us or someone else."

### Response Script B: The Confidence Play

**You:** "Absolutely — get as many quotes as you need to feel confident. I'll tell you what, though — after you've talked to everyone else, give me a call back. I'm confident we'll still be the best value when you compare the full picture. Here's my direct number."

### Response Script C: The Time Anchor

**You:** "No problem at all. Just keep in mind — our schedule is booking out [X weeks] right now, so if you decide to go with us after getting other quotes, the sooner you let me know, the sooner we can get you on the calendar."

## 4. I Need to Think About It

### Psychology

"Think about it" is a catch-all for "something is unresolved but I don't want to get into it." There is always a specific concern underneath. Your job is to gently surface it.

The most common hidden concerns:
- They're worried about the money but embarrassed to say so
- They're not sure the problem is really that serious
- They don't fully trust you or your diagnosis
- They want to compare but don't want to say they're shopping

### What NOT to Say

- "What's there to think about?" — Dismissive and confrontational.
- "This price is only good today." — Almost always a lie. Customers know it. Trust destroyed.
- "Take all the time you need!" (and leave) — You just lost the deal. Follow up will feel cold.
- "I'll call you in a few days." — No urgency, no specificity, no commitment from either side.

### Response Script A: Binary Clarification

**You:** "Of course — take your time. Usually when someone tells me that, it's one of two things: they're not sure about the money, or they're not sure about the decision. Which one is it for you?"

**If money:** "Would it help if we looked at a payment plan? The Better option comes out to [$X/month] with financing."

**If decision:** "What part feels unclear or risky right now? I want to make sure you have all the information you need."

### Response Script B: Identify the Blocker

**You:** "Absolutely fair. Can I ask — on a scale of 1 to 10, where are you right now on moving forward? ... Okay, a [6/7/whatever]. What would it take to get you to a 9?"

**Why this works:** It quantifies their hesitation and then asks them to tell you exactly what they need. Most people will surface the real objection.

### Response Script C: The Permission Close

**You:** "I respect that — this is a big decision. Let me leave you with the written proposal and the photos. Before I go, though — is there anything about the solution itself that you're unsure about, or is it really just timing?"

**If timing:** "When would be a good time for me to check back in?"

**If solution:** Address the specific concern, then re-ask.

## 5. Can You Do Better on Price?

### Psychology

This is a negotiation reflex. Most people who ask this don't actually expect a discount — they're testing to see if there's room. How you respond determines whether they respect your pricing or think you were padding it.

### What NOT to Say

- "Sure, let me see what I can take off." — You just confirmed your price was inflated.
- "That's already our best price." — Flat, unhelpful, conversation-ender.
- "I can't do anything on price." — Feels rigid and uncaring.
- Any immediate discount without trade — devalues your work and makes them wonder how much margin you have.

### Response Script A: Diagnose the Ask

**You:** "Are you looking to lower the monthly payment, the total price, or are you still kind of feeling everything out?"

**If monthly payment:** "Great — let me show you the financing options. We can get this down to [$X/month], which is pretty manageable."

**If total price:** "I can't lower the price — our pricing reflects doing the job correctly with quality materials and a warranty we stand behind. But here's what I can do — I'll include [added value: extra year of maintenance, upgraded component, additional service] at no extra charge."

**If feeling it out:** "Let me ask — what part of the proposal feels like it's not quite right? Is it the scope, the price, or something else?"

### Response Script B: Value Stack

**You:** "Let me walk you through what's included in that number, because I want to make sure you see the full picture..."

[Re-list everything: materials, labor, permits, warranty, cleanup, post-install inspection, support]

"When you add all that up, the price actually represents a strong value. Would you agree?"

### Response Script C: The Trade

**You:** "I can't lower the price, but I could do one of these: include your first year of maintenance at no charge, extend the labor warranty by a year, or throw in [specific add-on]. Which of those would be most valuable to you?"

**Golden rule:** Never discount without a trade. Adding value is always better than reducing price.

## 6. Your Competitor Quoted Me Less

### Psychology

This is the sharpest objection because it involves a specific number. The customer isn't theorizing — they have a lower price in hand. But the lower price almost always comes with trade-offs they don't know about yet.

### What NOT to Say

- "They're going to cut corners." — Badmouthing. Even if true, it makes you look petty.
- "You get what you pay for." — Cliche. Not helpful.
- "We'll match it." — You just lost all pricing integrity. If you can match, why didn't you start there?
- "Their price doesn't include..." — Sounds like sour grapes unless you can prove it.

### Response Script A: The Fair Comparison

**You:** "That's good — it's smart to compare. Can I ask what's included in their quote? Specifically — are they pulling permits, is the equipment the same brand and model, what's the warranty on labor vs. parts, and what does their post-install support look like?"

**[Usually, they don't know the specifics]**

**You:** "Here's what I'd suggest — get those details from them in writing. Then let's compare side by side. I want you to make the best decision, and the only way to do that is to compare everything, not just the bottom line number."

### Response Script B: The Confidence Frame

**You:** "I appreciate you telling me that. I'm not going to badmouth them — there are good companies out there. What I can tell you is exactly what you get with us: [list specific differentiators — years in business, licensing, warranty, response time, quality of install]."

"A lower price might save you money today. The question is whether it saves you money over the next 10 years."

### Response Script C: The Risk Question

**You:** "If both quotes covered exactly the same work, same equipment, same warranty, and same quality — I'd say go with the cheaper one too. But in my experience, a big price gap usually means something is different. Can I show you specifically what's in our quote so you know what to ask them about?"

## 7. I Don't Trust Contractors

### Psychology

This is a deeply emotional objection rooted in a past experience — either theirs or someone close to them. It's not about you personally. It's about the entire industry. You cannot argue them out of this. You can only demonstrate trustworthiness through actions and transparency.

### What NOT to Say

- "We're not like other contractors." — Everyone says this. Meaningless.
- "That won't happen with us." — Unverifiable promise from a stranger.
- "I'm sorry that happened." — Empty without follow-through.
- Getting defensive in any way — they're not attacking you personally.

### Response Script A: Validation + Transparency

**You:** "I don't blame you. There are contractors out there who make the whole industry look bad. I've heard horror stories from customers and honestly, I hate it — it makes my job harder."

"Here's what I can do differently: I'm going to show you photos of everything I find, explain exactly what's going on in plain English, give you three options with the pricing broken down, and put everything in writing before any work starts. If at any point you feel uncomfortable, just say so."

### Response Script B: Social Proof

**You:** "I totally understand that feeling. Do you mind if I show you something? [Pull up Google reviews on your phone or tablet]. These are from homeowners in [your area] — people like you who had the same concern. I think their words say more than mine can."

"I can also give you the number of a few recent customers who'd be happy to share their experience. Would that help?"

### Response Script C: Empowerment

**You:** "Tell you what — I'm going to do my inspection and show you exactly what I find. Photos, video, the whole thing. Then I'll give you options with prices in writing. You don't have to decide today, you don't have to decide this week. No pressure at all. I just want to earn your trust — and that starts with being honest about what I find, even if it means telling you nothing is wrong."

## 8. I Want to Wait Until Next Season

### Psychology

Procrastination disguised as planning. They know they need the work done but the pain isn't acute enough to act now. Sometimes it's financial — they want time to save up. Sometimes it's just inertia.

### What NOT to Say

- "You really can't wait." — Sounds like a scare tactic unless backed by genuine safety data.
- "Prices will be higher next season." — May or may not be true. If false, destroys trust.
- "We're slow right now so we can give you a better deal." — Tells them you're desperate.
- Pressuring them aggressively — they said they want to wait, so respect that while gently educating.

### Response Script A: Cost of Waiting

**You:** "I totally understand wanting to time it right. A few things to think about, though — [specific consequence of waiting: 'running a failing system all summer costs you $X/month in efficiency,' 'that leak is doing about $X/month in hidden damage,' 'pest populations triple by spring if we don't treat now']."

"It's your call, but I'd rather you know the full picture before you decide to wait."

### Response Script B: The Seasonal Advantage

**You:** "Actually, [current season] is a great time to do this because [legitimate reason: 'our schedule is more flexible, so we can usually get you done faster,' 'manufacturers are running rebates right now,' 'the weather is cooperating, which means a better install']."

"If you wait until [peak season], everyone is calling at the same time and lead times can stretch to [X] weeks."

### Response Script C: Lock It In Now, Schedule Later

**You:** "What if we did this — lock in today's price and schedule the work for whenever works best for you? That way you get the current pricing without any rush. We can book it for [their preferred timeframe] and you're all set."

## 9. Can You Just Do a Temporary Fix?

### Psychology

They want the cheapest possible solution to make the problem go away — at least for now. This usually comes from budget anxiety or a belief that the "real" fix isn't worth it. Sometimes it's a test to see if you're going to upsell them.

### What NOT to Say

- "No, you need the full replacement." — Makes you sound like you don't care about their budget.
- "A temporary fix is a waste of money." — Dismissive of what they're asking for.
- "I wouldn't recommend that." — Too vague. WHY wouldn't you recommend it?

### Response Script A: Good Tier as the Temporary Option

**You:** "Absolutely — that's actually what the Good option covers. It handles the immediate issue safely and gets you back up and running. It's [$X]. The reason I also showed you the Better and Best options is so you can see what a longer-term solution looks like. But if right now you just need it fixed, the Good option does that."

### Response Script B: Honest Assessment

**You:** "I can do a temporary repair — it would be [$X] and it should last [realistic timeframe]. But I want to be honest with you: you'll probably be back in [timeframe] dealing with the same thing. The total cost of patching this twice is actually more than fixing it right the first time."

"I'm not trying to upsell you — I just don't want you to spend money now and end up spending more later. What makes the most sense for your situation?"

### Response Script C: The Written Comparison

**You:** "Let me show you both paths side by side."

| Option | Cost Now | Expected Lifespan | Cost Over 5 Years |
|--------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Temporary repair | $X | 6-12 months | $X x 3-4 repairs = $Y |
| Permanent fix | $Z | 10-15+ years | $Z (one time) |

"When you see it this way, the permanent fix is actually the cheaper option. But the temporary repair buys you time if that's what you need right now."

## 10. I Read Online That...

### Psychology

The internet-educated customer. They've done "research" — YouTube, Reddit, forums, home improvement blogs. They may have accurate information, partially accurate information, or completely wrong information. This objection is really about control — they don't want to feel like the ignorant homeowner being taken advantage of by the expert.

### What NOT to Say

- "You can't believe everything you read online." — Condescending. Guaranteed to make them defensive.
- "Those articles are written by people who've never done this." — Dismissive of their effort.
- "That's wrong." — Even if it is, saying it bluntly creates conflict.
- Eye-rolling, sighing, or any body language that suggests you think they're wasting your time.

### Response Script A: Validate and Educate

**You:** "That's actually a great question — I've seen that article/video too. Here's what they get right: [acknowledge what's accurate]. Where it gets tricky is [explain the nuance they're missing]."

"The thing about online info is it's usually about a specific situation. Your home has [specific variable] that changes things. Let me show you what I mean..."

### Response Script B: Expertise Demonstration

**You:** "You've clearly done your homework — I appreciate that. Let me add some context based on what I've seen in [X] years of doing this..."

[Explain the real-world nuance — not to prove them wrong, but to add depth to what they know]

"Does that help fill in the picture?"

### Response Script C: The Collaborative Approach

**You:** "I love it when homeowners research before we come out — it makes the conversation so much easier. Based on what you've read, what concerns you most about [the recommended solution]?"

[Address their specific concern]

"Here's what I'd suggest — let me do a full inspection and show you what I find. Then we can talk through whether what you read applies to your specific situation. Fair?"

## 11. The Last Company Ripped Me Off

### Psychology

This is a trauma response. They were financially or emotionally hurt by a previous contractor. They're approaching your interaction through a lens of distrust and vigilance. They may be hostile, extremely detail-oriented, or seemingly impossible to please — all of which are defense mechanisms.

This is NOT about you. Do not take it personally. Your job is to be the opposite of whatever they experienced.

### What NOT to Say

- "That sucks." — Too casual for their pain.
- "We're way better than them." — Unverifiable, sounds like a sales pitch.
- "You should report them to the BBB." — Unsolicited advice, and it doesn't solve their current problem.
- Anything that minimizes their experience.

### Response Script A: Full Empathy + Concrete Differentiation

**You:** "That's terrible — and unfortunately, I hear that more often than I should. It makes me angry because it gives our whole industry a bad name. Can I ask what happened?"

[Listen fully. Don't interrupt. Let them vent.]

**You:** "Thank you for sharing that. Here's what I can promise you: I'll put every detail in writing before any work starts. The price I quote is the price you pay — no surprises. And you'll have my direct cell number — not a call center. If anything isn't right, you call me and I'll personally come back and fix it."

### Response Script B: Show, Don't Tell

**You:** "I can tell that experience left a bad taste, and I totally get it. Instead of me telling you how we're different — because everyone says that — let me show you. I'm going to do my inspection, take photos and video of everything, explain it in plain English, and give you a written proposal with three options. No pressure, no rush."

"After that, you can check our reviews, call our references, or even talk to your neighbors who've used us. I don't need you to trust me right now — I just need a chance to earn it."

### Response Script C: The Guarantee Offer

**You:** "After what you went through, I don't blame you for being cautious. Here's what I'll do: I'll put a satisfaction guarantee in writing. If you're not 100% happy with the work, we come back and make it right — no questions, no extra charge. And I'll put my name and direct number on the invoice. Not a company line. My personal number."

"I can't undo what happened with the last company, but I can make sure it never happens again."
