# Pricing & Job Costing for Local Service Businesses

## 1. Core Principles

Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. A business doing $2 million in revenue with 5% margins ($100K) is worse off than one doing $800K with 25% margins ($200K). Stop chasing revenue. Chase profit.

Most contractors undercharge because they confuse markup with margin. A 25% markup is NOT a 25% margin. This single misunderstanding bankrupts more service businesses than any other financial mistake. Section 2 below is the most important section in this entire skill.

Know your numbers BEFORE you quote. Every job you price without knowing your true costs is a gamble. You might make money. You might lose money. You won't know until it's too late. Guessing is gambling, and the house always wins eventually.

Price for profit, not for busy. Being busy and broke is worse than being slow and profitable. If you're running crews six days a week and still can't make payroll comfortably, your pricing is broken. Full schedules with thin margins just means you're doing charity work with extra steps.

Your price communicates your value. When you charge $49 for a service call, you're telling the customer you're worth $49. When the company down the street charges $89 for the same call and shows up in a clean truck with a uniformed tech who puts on shoe covers, the customer sees the difference. Cheap prices attract cheap customers who leave bad reviews when anything goes wrong.

Review and adjust pricing at least annually. Costs go up every single year — insurance, fuel, materials, wages. If your prices stay flat, your margins shrink. A business that doesn't raise prices is giving itself a pay cut every year.

The goal is not to be the cheapest. The goal is to be so good that your price is irrelevant.

## 2. Markup vs Margin — The Most Common Mistake

This is the single most important concept in this skill. Most service business owners get this wrong, and it costs them thousands of dollars every month without them realizing it.

**Markup** is how much you add ON TOP of your cost.
**Margin** is what percentage of the SELLING PRICE is profit.

They are NOT the same number.

### Conversion Table

| Markup % | Actual Margin % | Reality Check |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| 10% | 9.1% | Barely covers overhead. You are losing money. |
| 15% | 13.0% | Still underwater for virtually all service businesses. |
| 20% | 16.7% | Still losing money for most trades after overhead. |
| 25% | 20.0% | Might break even if overhead is very low. |
| 30% | 23.1% | Breaking even for most trades. Not profitable yet. |
| 35% | 25.9% | Slim profit if everything goes right. |
| 40% | 28.6% | Starting to profit. Minimum viable for most. |
| 50% | 33.3% | Healthy for most service businesses. |
| 67% | 40.0% | Target for premium service companies. |
| 75% | 42.9% | Strong margins. Reinvestment possible. |
| 100% | 50.0% | High-end, specialized, or emergency work. |

### The Formulas

**Markup:**
```
Markup % = ((Selling Price - Cost) / Cost) x 100
```

**Margin:**
```
Margin % = ((Selling Price - Cost) / Selling Price) x 100
```

**Convert markup to margin:**
```
Margin = Markup / (1 + Markup)
Example: 50% markup = 0.50 / 1.50 = 33.3% margin
```

**Convert margin to markup:**
```
Markup = Margin / (1 - Margin)
Example: 40% margin = 0.40 / 0.60 = 66.7% markup
```

**Selling price from desired margin:**
```
Selling Price = Cost / (1 - Desired Margin)
Example: $1,000 cost, want 35% margin = $1,000 / 0.65 = $1,538
```

**Selling price from desired markup:**
```
Selling Price = Cost x (1 + Desired Markup)
Example: $1,000 cost, 50% markup = $1,000 x 1.50 = $1,500
```

### The Example That Changes Everything

A job costs $1,000 in direct costs (materials + labor). The owner adds 25% markup.

$1,000 x 1.25 = $1,250 selling price.

The owner thinks they made 25%. They actually made:
($250 / $1,250) x 100 = 20% margin.

After overhead (typically 15-20% for service businesses), that 20% margin becomes 0-5% net profit. On a $1,250 job, that's $0 to $62.50 in actual profit. For a job that took half a day.

To actually achieve a 25% margin:
$1,000 / (1 - 0.25) = $1,333 selling price. That requires a 33.3% markup.

To achieve a 35% margin (healthy profit):
$1,000 / (1 - 0.35) = $1,538 selling price. That requires a 53.8% markup.

The difference between "I marked up 25%" and "I need 35% margin" is $288 on a single job. Over 500 jobs a year, that's $144,000 left on the table.

### Quick Reference — "What Markup Do I Need?"

| If You Want This Margin | You Need This Markup |
|-------------------------|---------------------|
| 20% | 25.0% |
| 25% | 33.3% |
| 30% | 42.9% |
| 35% | 53.8% |
| 40% | 66.7% |
| 45% | 81.8% |
| 50% | 100.0% |

## 3. Know Your True Costs

Every dollar you don't account for comes directly out of your profit. Most service businesses undercount their costs, which means they underprice their work, which means they wonder why they're busy but broke.

### A. Direct Costs (Per Job)

These vary by job. Calculate for each quote.

- **Materials and parts** — Actual cost including tax, shipping, and any restocking risk. Not the retail price. Your cost from the supply house. Include the small stuff (fittings, connectors, tape, wire nuts). They add up.
- **Labor** — Hourly fully-burdened rate x hours. Fully burdened means wages PLUS payroll taxes (7.65% FICA), workers comp insurance, health insurance, PTO, retirement match, training time, and uniform costs. A tech you pay $30/hour actually costs $38-45/hour fully burdened.
- **Drive time** — The tech is on the clock from the moment they leave the shop. Include windshield time in your labor calculation. Two hours of drive time on a three-hour job means you need to recover five hours of labor, not three.
- **Permits and fees** — Pass these through at cost or mark them up. Never eat them.
- **Equipment rental** — If the job needs a lift, trencher, or specialty tool, it's a direct cost.
- **Subcontractor costs** — Mark up subs. You're managing them, coordinating scheduling, and taking the warranty risk. 10-20% markup on subs is standard.
- **Disposal fees** — Dumpster rental, refrigerant recovery, hazmat disposal. Real costs that get forgotten.

### B. Overhead (Monthly Fixed Costs)

These happen whether you run one job or fifty. They must be spread across all jobs.

- Rent or mortgage on shop/office/yard
- Vehicle payments, fuel, insurance, maintenance, registration (per truck)
- Tools and equipment depreciation (replace over time, budget now)
- Insurance — general liability, workers comp, commercial auto, umbrella
- Office staff wages (dispatcher, bookkeeper, office manager) fully burdened
- Software subscriptions (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, QuickBooks, CRM, etc.)
- Marketing and advertising (Google Ads, LSA, SEO, wrapped trucks, yard signs)
- Phone system and internet
- Licensing fees and continuing education / certifications
- Uniforms, PPE, and consumable supplies
- Accounting and legal fees
- Loan payments and interest
- Association dues and memberships
- Bad debt (callbacks, warranty, write-offs — yes, this is a cost)

### C. Owner's Salary (The Forgotten Expense)

This is the cost most owners leave out, and it destroys their numbers.

Ask: "What would I have to pay someone to do everything I do?" That's the number. If you're the lead tech, dispatcher, salesman, bookkeeper, and manager, you're doing five jobs. Price your time into the business.

Include in owner's salary calculation:
- Base pay (what the role would cost on the open market)
- Self-employment tax (15.3% if structured as sole prop or partnership)
- Health insurance
- Retirement contributions
- Paid time off (yes, owners need vacations too)

If you don't put your salary in the numbers, your "profit" is just you paying yourself. When a real emergency happens or you want to take a week off, there's no actual money.

### D. Calculating Your Overhead Rate

Step 1: Add up ALL monthly overhead costs (Section B + Section C above).
Step 2: Determine your total billable hours per month.
Step 3: Divide.

**Billable hours calculation:**
- Total work hours per month: 8 hours/day x 22 days = 176 hours per tech
- Subtract non-billable time: meetings, training, drive time (first/last trip), callbacks, idle time
- Realistic billable hours: 55-70% of total = 97-123 hours per tech per month
- For a 3-tech shop: ~300-370 billable hours per month

**Example overhead rate:**
```
Monthly overhead:        $18,000
Owner's salary:           $8,000
Total fixed costs:       $26,000
Billable hours/month:       330
Overhead per billable hour: $78.79 (round to $79)
```

Now you know: every billable hour must recover $79 in overhead BEFORE you make any profit.

If your tech's fully burdened cost is $42/hour, your true cost per billable hour is:
$42 (labor) + $79 (overhead) = $121/hour.

Any rate you charge below $121/hour loses money. Period.

## 4. Pricing Models

### Time & Materials (T&M)

**How it works:** Charge an hourly rate plus actual material costs (usually with markup).

**Best for:** Diagnostic work, troubleshooting, complex repairs where scope is unknown, service calls.

**Pros:**
- Fair for complex or unpredictable jobs
- Low risk for the contractor (customer pays for actual time)
- Easy to calculate

**Cons:**
- Customer anxiety ("the meter is running")
- No incentive for efficiency (in customer's mind)
- Harder to close sales — customer can't compare apples to apples
- Revenue limited by hours in the day

**How to set your T&M rate:**
```
Fully burdened labor cost + Overhead per hour + Desired profit per hour = Hourly rate
$42 + $79 + $40 (target profit) = $161/hour (round to $159 or $165)
```

### Flat Rate (Fixed Price)

**How it works:** Published price for each defined service, regardless of time spent.

**Best for:** Defined-scope work — tune-ups, installations, standard repairs, maintenance visits.

**Pros:**
- Customer knows price before work begins (reduces objections)
- Rewards efficiency (faster tech = more profit)
- Higher average tickets
- Easier to sell — "This is the price" vs "It depends"
- Consistent revenue per service

**Cons:**
- Risk if job takes longer than expected
- Requires good data on average job times
- Must update prices as costs change
- Complex jobs still need custom quoting

**Recommended mix:** Flat rate for 80% of work (defined service catalog), T&M for diagnostics and complex repairs that can't be scoped in advance.

### Building a Flat-Rate Price Book

1. Pull data from last 20 completions of each service type
2. Calculate average time (use the median, not the mean — outliers skew the mean)
3. Calculate average materials cost for that service
4. Apply overhead allocation
5. Apply desired profit margin (30-50%)
6. Round to a clean, professional number
7. Review quarterly against actual job costs
8. Adjust when average cost shifts more than 5%

**Example — HVAC capacitor replacement:**
```
Average tech time: 45 minutes (0.75 hours)
Labor cost (burdened): $42 x 0.75 = $31.50
Materials (capacitor + misc): $35
Overhead allocation: $79 x 0.75 = $59.25
Total cost: $125.75
Desired margin: 40%
Selling price: $125.75 / (1 - 0.40) = $209.58
Rounded flat rate: $209
```

### Cost-Plus

**How it works:** Total up all costs, add a fixed percentage markup.

**Best for:** Large custom projects, new construction, commercial contracts, remodels.

**Pros:**
- Simple and transparent
- Easy for customer to verify
- Low risk if costs are tracked accurately

**Cons:**
- Doesn't reward efficiency
- Customers may negotiate the markup down
- Open-book pricing means less perceived value

**Typical cost-plus rates:** 15-25% for commercial, 20-40% for residential, depending on project size and complexity.

### Value-Based Pricing

**How it works:** Price based on the value of the outcome to the customer, not the cost to you.

**Best for:** Emergency service, specialized/rare skills, problems with expensive consequences if not fixed.

**Pros:**
- Highest possible margins
- Aligns price with customer's perceived value
- No ceiling on revenue

**Cons:**
- Harder to justify if customer pushes back
- Requires strong sales skills and confidence
- Not appropriate for commodity services

**Examples:**
- Burst pipe flooding a finished basement: The value isn't "fix a pipe." It's "stop $50,000 in water damage."
- AC fails during a heat wave with elderly residents: The value isn't "replace a contactor." It's "safety and comfort within the hour."
- Sewer backup before a holiday party: The value isn't "clear a drain." It's "save Thanksgiving."

## 6. Break-Even Analysis

Every business owner needs to know their break-even point — the exact number of jobs or revenue needed each month before they make a single dollar of profit.

### The Formula

```
Break-Even (in jobs) = Monthly Fixed Costs / (Average Revenue Per Job - Average Variable Cost Per Job)
```

```
Break-Even (in revenue) = Monthly Fixed Costs / Gross Margin Percentage
```

### Worked Example

**Monthly fixed costs:** $22,000 (rent, trucks, insurance, office staff, owner salary, etc.)
**Average job revenue:** $650
**Average variable cost per job:** $310 (materials, tech labor, drive time)
**Gross profit per job:** $650 - $310 = $340
**Gross margin:** $340 / $650 = 52.3%

**Break-even in jobs:** $22,000 / $340 = 64.7 = 65 jobs per month
**Break-even in revenue:** $22,000 / 0.523 = $42,065 per month

That means:
- Jobs 1-65 each month go to covering overhead. Zero profit.
- Job 66 and beyond is where profit starts.
- At 22 working days, you need to complete 3 jobs per day just to break even.

### Weekly Tracking

Don't wait until month-end to find out you missed break-even. Track weekly:

```
Week 1: Need ~16 jobs to be on pace → Actually completed: ___
Week 2: Cumulative need ~33 → Actually completed: ___
Week 3: Cumulative need ~49 → Actually completed: ___
Week 4: Must hit 65 → Actually completed: ___
```

If you're behind pace by mid-month, you need to act immediately:
- Push pending maintenance plan visits forward
- Increase marketing spend for the short term
- Offer schedule openings to customers who deferred work
- Review outstanding quotes and follow up

### Break-Even for New Service Lines

Before adding a new service (duct cleaning, water heater installs, generator service):
1. Estimate additional fixed costs (training, equipment, marketing)
2. Estimate revenue and margin per job
3. Calculate how many new jobs per month to cover the investment
4. Determine timeline to break even on the investment

If a $15,000 equipment investment is needed and each new service generates $200 in gross profit:
$15,000 / $200 = 75 jobs to recoup the investment.
At 8 jobs per month, that's ~9.5 months to break even.

## 7. Price Increase Strategy

Raising prices is the single fastest way to increase profit. A 5% price increase on a business doing $500K in revenue adds $25,000 straight to the bottom line (assuming you don't lose customers — and you won't lose as many as you think).

### When to Raise Prices

**Raise immediately when:**
- Material costs increased (pass through at next quote, not next quarter)
- Fuel costs spiked significantly
- Insurance premiums went up at renewal
- Minimum wage or prevailing wage increased

**Raise proactively when:**
- Annual review (minimum once per year, 3-5% to keep up with inflation)
- Your skills or certifications improved (new training, new capabilities)
- Demand exceeds capacity (if you're booked 3+ weeks out, you're too cheap)
- You added value (new trucks, new technology, better warranty, faster response)
- You haven't raised prices in 12+ months (you're falling behind right now)

**Raise strategically when:**
- Entering a new market segment
- Repositioning from budget to premium
- Launching a new service with no price anchor
- After a brand refresh or major quality improvement

### How Much to Raise

**Annual maintenance increase:** 3-5% (non-negotiable, covers inflation)
**Material cost pass-through:** Exact amount, documented
**Market adjustment:** Whatever it takes to reach top 25% in your market
**Value-based adjustment:** Proportional to new value delivered

Rule of thumb: If you raise prices 10% and lose 10% of your customers, you make the same revenue with less work. You're actually more profitable because your variable costs dropped with fewer jobs but your revenue held steady.

The math:
- Before: 100 jobs x $500 = $50,000 revenue
- After: 90 jobs x $550 = $49,500 revenue (nearly identical)
- But: 10% fewer jobs = less labor cost, less truck wear, less materials, less overhead
- Net result: More profit, less work, better customers

### Communication Framework

**Give 30 days notice minimum.** More for large commercial accounts.

**What to say:**
- "Our costs for materials, insurance, and fuel have increased over the past year. To continue providing the level of service you expect, we are adjusting our rates effective [date]."
- Never apologize. Never say "we want more money." Frame it as maintaining quality.
- For maintenance plan members: "As a valued plan member, your new rate is locked in at [discounted increase] for the next 12 months."
- For commercial contracts: "Per our agreement, this annual adjustment reflects the change in [CPI / materials index / labor rates]."

**What NOT to do:**
- Apologize profusely (this signals you think the increase isn't justified)
- Offer discounts preemptively (wait for them to ask, and even then, consider saying no)
- Hide the increase (customers notice; being upfront builds trust)
- Raise prices and reduce quality simultaneously

**Grandfathering:** Honor existing contracts and quotes through their term. New pricing applies to new work and renewals.

## 8. Competitive Pricing Analysis

### Research Methods

1. **Mystery shopping** — Call 3-5 competitors for quotes on common services. Ask the same questions each time. Note: price, professionalism, response time, how they handled the call. Document everything.

2. **Online research** — Search "[your service] cost in [your city]." Many competitors list price ranges on their websites. Check Google Business profiles for pricing hints in reviews ("They charged me $X for Y").

3. **Customer intel** — When a customer calls for a quote, ask: "Have you gotten any other estimates?" They'll usually share the number. Track this data over time — it builds your competitive pricing map.

4. **Industry benchmarks** — Trade associations publish annual pricing surveys:
   - ACCA (HVAC): cost survey and pricing guide
   - PHCC (Plumbing): labor and material pricing data
   - NECA (Electrical): labor rate surveys
   - NRCA (Roofing): annual market survey

5. **Cost guides** — HomeAdvisor, Angi, Thumbtack, and Yelp all publish regional cost guides. These reflect what consumers expect to pay, not what you should charge, but they set customer expectations.

### Market Positioning

| Position | Price Point | Strategy | Risk |
|----------|------------|----------|------|
| Bottom 25% | Lowest in market | Competing on price | Race to the bottom. First to go bankrupt. |
| Middle 50% | Market average | Competitive but undifferentiated | Commoditized. Customers choose on availability. |
| Top 25% | Premium pricing | Compete on value, not price | Must deliver premium experience to justify. |

**Where you should aim: Top 25%.** Charge more. Deliver more. Attract better customers.

### Compete on Value, Not Price

Things worth more than a low price:
- Fast response time (same-day, 2-hour windows)
- Professional appearance (clean trucks, uniforms, shoe covers, drop cloths)
- Clear communication (arrival texts, tech photos, follow-up calls)
- Strong warranty (2 years labor, not just manufacturer warranty)
- Transparent pricing (show options, explain clearly, no surprises)
- Online reviews (4.8+ stars with hundreds of reviews)
- Financing options (customer pays over time, you get paid now)
- Background-checked, drug-tested techs (peace of mind)

Every one of these justifies a higher price. And none of them require you to be a better technician — they require you to be a better business.

Use this for every job over $500 to track actual cost vs estimate. For smaller jobs, use the flat-rate price book and review monthly.

```
  Labor (___hrs x $___/hr):  $________

  Equipment rental:          $________
  Disposal / hauling:        $________
  Other:                     $________
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  TOTAL DIRECT COST:         $________

  Direct cost x ____% OR
  Hours x $____/hr:          $________
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  TOTAL COST (break-even):   $________

PRICING:
  Desired margin:               ____%
  Selling price (Cost / (1-margin)): $________
  Rounded selling price:     $________
```

### Post-Job Actuals

```
ACTUAL COSTS:
  Materials & parts:         $________
  Labor (___hrs x $___/hr):  $________
  Drive time:                $________
  Subcontractors:            $________
  Permits & fees:            $________
  Equipment rental:          $________
  Disposal / hauling:        $________
  Other:                     $________
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  TOTAL ACTUAL DIRECT COST:  $________

ACTUAL REVENUE:              $________

VARIANCE:
  Estimated direct cost:     $________
  Actual direct cost:        $________
  Difference:                $________ (over/under)

ACTUAL MARGIN:
  (Revenue - Actual Direct Cost - Overhead) / Revenue = ____%

NOTES:
  What went right: ___________________________________
  What went wrong: ___________________________________
  Adjust estimate for next time? Y/N: ________________
```

Track the variance on every job. Patterns reveal problems:
- Consistently underestimating labor hours? Your time estimates are wrong. Pull actual data.
- Materials always over estimate? You're forgetting small parts. Add a 10% materials buffer.
- Drive time eating margin? Route optimization or territory tightening needed.
- Callbacks on certain job types? Quality issue, not a pricing issue — but it affects profit.

## 10. Profitability Red Flags

Watch for these warning signs. Each one points to a specific problem.

### Red Flag: Busy but Not Profitable

**Symptom:** Schedule is full, trucks are rolling, revenue looks good, but bank account doesn't grow.
**Root cause:** Pricing too low. Revenue covers direct costs and overhead but leaves nothing.
**Fix:** Raise prices 10-15%. You'll lose some bottom-tier customers. That's the point.

### Red Flag: High Material Cost as % of Revenue

**Symptom:** Materials are 40%+ of job revenue (should be 15-30% for most trades).
**Root cause:** Not marking up materials enough, or eating material cost increases.
**Fix:** Increase material markup. Pass through cost increases immediately, not at next price review.

### Red Flag: Techs Taking Too Long

**Symptom:** Jobs consistently take 30-50% longer than estimated.
**Root cause:** Training issue, not a pricing issue. But it destroys margins just the same.
**Fix:** Ride-alongs to observe workflow. Identify bottlenecks. Train or reassign. Don't just raise the price to cover inefficiency.

### Red Flag: Too Many Callbacks

**Symptom:** 5%+ callback rate (same customer, same issue, within 30 days).
**Root cause:** Quality problem. Rushing, wrong diagnosis, or bad parts.
**Fix:** Callbacks cost you the full labor of the return trip plus the goodwill damage. Track callbacks by tech and by job type. Address the root cause. A 1% reduction in callbacks on 1,000 jobs/year at $200 average cost = $2,000 saved.

### Red Flag: Owner Working 60+ Hours

**Symptom:** Owner is the first one in, last one out, doing every role.
**Root cause:** Owner's salary isn't in the numbers. The business can't afford to hire because it was never priced to support the headcount it actually needs.
**Fix:** Put owner's salary in the overhead calculation. Reprice everything. Then start hiring.

### Red Flag: Growing Revenue but Flat Profit

**Symptom:** Revenue up 20% year over year. Profit flat or declining.
**Root cause:** Scaling at wrong margins. Every new dollar of revenue comes with nearly a dollar of cost. More trucks, more techs, more overhead, same thin margins.
**Fix:** Fix margins first, then scale. Growing a business with 5% margins just creates a bigger problem.

### Red Flag: Discounting to Win Jobs

**Symptom:** Techs or salespeople regularly discount 10-20% to close. "The customer said your competitor is cheaper."
**Root cause:** Lack of sales training and value communication. Also possibly a positioning problem.
**Fix:** Train on value selling (see sales-scripts skill — GBB framework). Remove discount authority from techs. If a customer needs a lower price, offer a reduced scope, not a reduced margin.

### Red Flag: No Difference Between Best and Worst Jobs

**Symptom:** All jobs produce roughly the same margin whether they're big or small.
**Root cause:** Not tracking job-level profitability. Flat pricing applied without regard to actual cost variation.
**Fix:** Implement job costing (Section 9). Identify your most and least profitable job types. Do more of what makes money. Fix or drop what doesn't.
