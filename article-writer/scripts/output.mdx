Manufacturing and selling products through Amazon offers enormous revenue potential, but it also introduces liability exposure that can threaten your entire business if left unaddressed. Amazon has strict insurance requirements for sellers, and understanding exactly what coverage you need — and when — is critical before listing your first unit. This guide breaks down the platform's insurance policies, the liability risks manufacturers face, and how to secure the right policy to protect your operations and stay compliant.

## Why Amazon Requires Product Liability Insurance

Amazon processes billions of dollars in third-party sales annually, and a significant portion of those transactions involve physical goods that can cause injury, property damage, or allergic reactions. To protect both the platform and consumers, Amazon mandates that certain sellers carry **commercial general liability insurance** that includes product liability coverage. According to the [Insurance Information Institute's guide to business insurance](https://www.iii.org/article/what-is-business-insurance), product liability coverage is one of the foundational protections any company that manufactures, distributes, or sells physical goods should maintain — and Amazon enforces this standard at the platform level.

The policy exists because Amazon has been named in lawsuits over defective products sold by third-party sellers. Courts have at times held the platform partially responsible for items in its fulfillment network, which accelerated Amazon's push to require sellers to demonstrate financial responsibility. By insisting on insurance, Amazon transfers a portion of that risk back to the manufacturers and brands who are best positioned to control product quality.

## Amazon's Insurance Threshold and Requirements

Amazon's seller insurance policy applies once you reach a specific revenue threshold, and the requirements are clearly documented in the platform's Seller Central terms. Here are the key details:

- **Revenue trigger**: You must obtain insurance once your cumulative gross revenue on Amazon exceeds **$10,000 in any calendar month**.
- **Coverage minimum**: At least **$1 million per occurrence** in commercial general liability coverage.
- **Additional insured**: Amazon.com Services, Inc. and its affiliates must be listed as **additional insureds** on your policy.
- **Accepted insurers**: The policy must be issued by an insurer with an A.M. Best financial strength rating of A- or better, or equivalent.
- **Proof of coverage**: You must upload a certificate of insurance (COI) through Seller Central within the timeframe Amazon specifies after crossing the revenue threshold.

Failure to provide proof of insurance can result in your listings being deactivated or your seller account being suspended entirely. The [SBA's guide to business insurance](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/get-business-insurance) notes that many small manufacturers underestimate the urgency of securing coverage until a marketplace or retail partner demands it — by which point compliance deadlines may already be looming.

### Product-Specific Considerations

Certain product categories carry additional scrutiny. Items intended for children, electronics with lithium batteries, dietary supplements, cosmetics, and any product that has been subject to a safety recall face more rigorous insurance and documentation requirements. The [Consumer Product Safety Commission's recall guidance](https://www.cpsc.gov/Business--Manufacturing/Recall-Guidance) outlines how manufacturers should respond to safety concerns, and Amazon cross-references CPSC recall databases when evaluating seller risk.

If your product has ever been recalled — or is in a category prone to recalls — your insurer may require higher coverage limits or additional endorsements. Planning for this during the product development phase, rather than after you have inventory in Amazon fulfillment centers, will save both time and money.

## What Product Liability Insurance Actually Covers

Before purchasing a policy, manufacturers should understand what product liability insurance protects against. [Commercial general liability insurance](https://www.iii.org/article/commercial-general-liability-insurance) typically includes bodily injury and property damage coverage arising from products you manufacture, distribute, or sell. The three primary categories of claims are:

- **Manufacturing defects**: A flaw in the production process that makes a product unsafe — for example, a batch of power tools with improperly wired components that short-circuit.
- **Design defects**: An inherent flaw in the product's design that makes it dangerous even when manufactured correctly — such as a chair with a leg angle that causes it to tip under normal use.
- **Marketing defects**: Failures in labeling, instructions, or warnings that lead to improper use and injury — like cleaning chemicals sold without adequate safety warnings.

Our article on [the three types of product liability claims manufacturers face](/blog/three-types-of-product-liability-claims-manufacturers-face) explores each category in greater depth. Understanding these distinctions matters because different defects trigger different policy responses, and some exclusions may apply depending on the nature of the claim.

### Coverage Gaps to Watch For

Standard product liability policies do not cover every scenario. Common exclusions include:

- **Product recalls**: The cost of pulling defective products from the market, including logistics, customer notifications, and replacement shipments, is generally not covered under a standard CGL policy. You need a separate product recall endorsement or a standalone recall policy. Our guide on [product recall insurance](/blog/product-recall-insurance-what-manufacturers-need-to-know) explains what manufacturers should look for in recall-specific coverage.

- **Intentional misconduct**: If a manufacturer knowingly ships defective products, the insurer will likely deny the claim and may void the policy entirely.

- **Contractual liability beyond policy terms**: If you sign a vendor agreement with Amazon that requires coverage limits or endorsements exceeding what your policy provides, you are out of compliance regardless of what your insurer covers.

Reviewing your policy alongside Amazon's vendor requirements and the [FTC's business guidance on product claims](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance) is essential to ensure you are not overpromising in your listings while underinsuring against the consequences.

## How Much Coverage Do You Actually Need?

Amazon's $1 million per-occurrence minimum is a floor, not a recommendation. For many manufacturers, $1 million is insufficient given the scale of Amazon distribution and the severity of potential claims. Consider these factors when determining your coverage limits:

- **Annual revenue on Amazon**: Higher sales volume means more units in circulation and more exposure to claims.
- **Product risk profile**: Products used by children, ingested orally, or installed in homes (like electrical components) carry higher severity risk than novelty items.
- **Geographic distribution**: Amazon ships nationally and internationally, which means your products may be subject to litigation in jurisdictions with different liability standards and damage awards.

Our article on [how much product liability insurance manufacturers need](/blog/how-much-product-liability-insurance-do-you-need) provides a detailed framework for calculating appropriate limits based on revenue, product type, and distribution reach. Many manufacturers selling at scale on Amazon carry limits of $2 million to $5 million per occurrence to account for class-action exposure and multi-state litigation.

The [OSHA business case for safety investments](https://www.osha.gov/businesscase) demonstrates that proactive quality control and workplace safety programs reduce both the frequency and severity of product defects — which in turn reduces insurance premiums over time. Insurers offer better rates to manufacturers who can document robust quality management systems, incoming material inspections, and post-sale monitoring processes.

## Steps to Secure the Right Policy for Amazon Sales

Getting insured for Amazon sales requires coordinating between your insurance broker, your legal or compliance team, and Amazon Seller Central. Follow this sequence:

1. **Audit your current coverage**: Review any existing general liability or product liability policies to determine whether they already meet Amazon's minimum requirements — $1 million per occurrence, $1 million aggregate, with Amazon listed as an additional insured.

2. **Identify coverage gaps**: Compare your current limits against your revenue, product risk, and Amazon's contractual requirements. If you sell high-risk products or generate significant monthly revenue, plan for limits above the minimum.

3. **Work with a specialized broker**: Generalist insurance agents may not understand the nuances of manufacturing liability or Amazon's specific endorsement language. A broker experienced with manufacturer and e-commerce clients will secure better terms and ensure compliance documentation is formatted correctly for Seller Central.

4. **Upload your certificate of insurance promptly**: Once your policy is in place, upload the COI through Amazon Seller Central immediately. Amazon may deactivate listings if the COI is not provided within the specified window after you cross the $10,000 monthly revenue threshold.

5. **Set renewal reminders**: Insurance lapses can trigger immediate account suspension. Track your policy expiration date and initiate renewal discussions at least 60 days in advance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is product liability insurance legally required to sell on Amazon?

Amazon mandates commercial general liability insurance with product liability coverage once your monthly sales exceed $10,000. While no federal law universally requires it, the platform will deactivate listings and suspend your account without proof of adequate coverage, making it effectively mandatory for any serious Amazon seller.

### How much does Amazon product liability insurance cost for manufacturers?

Costs vary based on product type, annual revenue, claims history, and coverage limits. Manufacturers of low-risk consumer goods may pay $500 to $2,000 annually for $1 million in coverage, while high-risk categories like electronics, supplements, or children's products can cost $5,000 or more per year.

### Can I use my existing business insurance policy for Amazon sales?

You may be able to if your current policy meets Amazon's minimums — $1 million per occurrence, an A-rated insurer, and Amazon listed as an additional insured. However, many standard business policies exclude product liability or have product-specific exclusions, so review your policy carefully with your broker.

### What happens if I sell on Amazon without insurance?

Once you exceed $10,000 in monthly revenue, Amazon will request proof of insurance. If you fail to provide it within the specified timeframe, Amazon can deactivate your listings and suspend your seller account. Additionally, selling uninsured leaves you personally exposed to lawsuits, legal fees, and damages that could bankrupt your business.

### Does Amazon's insurance requirement apply to all product categories?

The $10,000 revenue threshold applies broadly, but certain high-risk categories — children's products, electronics, cosmetics, supplements, and items with known safety recalls — face heightened scrutiny. Amazon may require insurance earlier or demand additional documentation regardless of revenue for products in these categories.
